Minerals are essential substances that play a critical role in both human life and geological processes. They are found embedded within the earth's crust and serve as the fundamental building blocks for various materials, from everyday household items to massive industrial structures.

## Definitions

- **Minerals:** Geologists define a mineral as a "homogenous, naturally occurring substance with a definable internal structure." Minerals vary widely in their physical properties, ranging from the extreme hardness of a diamond to the extreme softness of talc.
- **Rocks:** Rocks are combinations of homogenous substances known as minerals. While some rocks, such as limestone, consist of only a single mineral, most rocks are composed of several different minerals in varying proportions.
- **Ores:** An ore is an accumulation of any mineral mixed with other elements. For a mineral to be considered an ore, its concentration must be high enough to make extraction commercially viable.

## Perspectives on Mineral Study

The study of minerals differs depending on the scientific discipline:
- **Geographers** study minerals as part of the earth's crust to better understand landforms and the distribution of mineral resources and associated economic activities.
- **Geologists** focus on the formation of minerals, their age, and their physical and chemical composition.

## Modes of Occurrence

The structure or formation in which minerals are found determines the ease and cost of mining. Minerals generally occur in the following three forms:

- **Veins and Lodes:** In igneous and metamorphic rocks, minerals may occur in cracks, crevices, faults, or joints. Smaller occurrences are called **veins**, while larger ones are called **lodes**. These are typically formed when molten or gaseous minerals are forced upward through cavities toward the surface, cooling and solidifying as they rise. Major metallic minerals like tin, copper, zinc, and lead are often obtained this way.
- **Beds or Layers:** In sedimentary rocks, minerals occur in horizontal strata due to deposition, accumulation, and concentration. 
    - Some, like coal and certain iron ores, are concentrated through long periods of heat and pressure.
    - Others, such as gypsum, potash salt, and sodium salt, are formed through evaporation, particularly in arid regions.
- **Residual Mass:** This mode involves the decomposition of surface rocks and the removal of soluble constituents, leaving behind a weathered mass that contains ores. Bauxite is a primary example of a mineral formed this way.

**Key facts:**
- **Mineral variety:** Over 2,000 minerals have been identified.
- **Classification criteria:** Geologists classify minerals based on properties such as color, hardness, crystal form, lustre, and density.
- **Biological importance:** While minerals make up only about 0.3% of total nutrient intake, they are essential for life processes and for utilizing other foodstuffs.
- **Common mineral uses:** 
    - **Toothpaste:** Silica, limestone, aluminium oxide, phosphate minerals, fluorite (for fluoride), titanium oxide (from rutile, ilmenite, or anatase), and mica.
    - **Plastics:** Derived from petroleum.
