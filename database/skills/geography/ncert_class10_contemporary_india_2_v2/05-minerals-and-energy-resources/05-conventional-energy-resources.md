Conventional energy resources in India primarily consist of fossil fuels, including coal, petroleum, and natural gas. These non-renewable sources are essential for power generation, industrial processes, and domestic needs.

## Coal

Coal is India's most abundant fossil fuel and a critical component of its commercial energy requirements. It is formed from the compression of plant material over millions of years. The type of coal produced depends on the degree of compression, depth, and time of burial.

### Types of Coal
- **Peat:** Produced from decaying plants in swamps; characterized by low carbon, high moisture, and low heating capacity.
- **Lignite:** A low-grade brown coal that is soft with high moisture content. Major reserves are located in Neyveli, Tamil Nadu.
- **Bituminous:** Formed from coal buried deep under high temperatures; it is the most common coal used in commercial applications.
- **Metallurgical Coal:** A high-grade variety of bituminous coal used specifically for smelting iron in blast furnaces.
- **Anthracite:** The highest quality hard coal.

### Distribution in India
Coal in India occurs in two main geological ages:
- **Gondwana Period:** Over 200 million years old. Major resources, including metallurgical coal, are found in the Damodar Valley (West Bengal and Jharkhand), including important coalfields like Jharia, Raniganj, and Bokaro. Other deposits are found in the Godavari, Mahanadi, Son, and Wardha valleys.
- **Tertiary Period:** Approximately 55 million years old. These deposits are found in the north-eastern states, including Meghalaya, Assam, Arunachal Pradesh, and Nagaland.

Because coal is a bulky material that reduces to ash upon use, heavy industries and thermal power stations are typically located near coalfields.

## Petroleum

Petroleum (mineral oil) is the second major energy source in India. It serves as fuel for heat and lighting, a lubricant for machinery, and a raw material for various industries. Petroleum refineries function as "nodal industries" for the chemical, fertilizer, and synthetic textile sectors.

### Geological Occurrence
Most petroleum occurrences in India are associated with tertiary age rock formations, specifically in:
- **Anticlines and Domes:** Oil is trapped in the crest of upfolds within porous limestone or sandstone layers, prevented from moving by non-porous layers.
- **Fault Traps:** Occurs between porous and non-porous rocks.
- **Gas Placement:** Since gas is lighter than oil, it usually occurs above the petroleum layer.

### Major Production Areas
- **Mumbai High:** A major offshore field.
- **Gujarat:** The Ankeleshwar field is the most important in this state.
- **Assam:** The oldest oil-producing state in India, featuring fields such as Digboi, Naharkatiya, and Moran-Hugrijan.

## Natural Gas

Natural gas is often found alongside petroleum deposits and is released during the extraction of crude oil. It is a versatile fuel used for domestic, industrial, and power sector applications.

- **Uses:** Power generation, industrial heating, raw material for petrochemicals and fertilizers, transport fuel (CNG), and cooking fuel (PNG).
- **Major Reserves:** Found in Mumbai High and allied fields along the west coast, the Cambay basin, and the Krishna-Godavari basin on the east coast.
- **Infrastructure:** GAIL (India) constructed the 1,700 km Hazira-Vijaipur-Jagdishpur (HVJ) pipeline to link Mumbai High and Bassein gas fields to industrial complexes. India's gas pipeline infrastructure has expanded from 1,700 km to 18,500 km and is expected to reach 34,000 km.

**Key facts:**
- **Coal types (low to high quality):** Peat $\rightarrow$ Lignite $\rightarrow$ Bituminous $\rightarrow$ Anthracite.
- **Oldest oil-producing state:** Assam.
- **Major coal geological ages:** Gondwana (~200 million years) and Tertiary (~55 million years).
- **Key gas pipeline operator:** GAIL (India).
