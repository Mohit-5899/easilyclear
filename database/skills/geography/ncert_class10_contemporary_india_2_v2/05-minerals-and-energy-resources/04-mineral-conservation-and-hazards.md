Mining is a critical industrial activity, but it presents significant risks and challenges regarding resource depletion and safety. Because mineral formation is a slow geological process, the industry must balance extraction with conservation and safety regulations.

## Mining Hazards and Safety

To prevent mining from becoming a "killer industry," there is a critical need for:
- Stricter safety regulations.
- Effective implementation of environmental laws.

## Conservation of Minerals

Mineral resources are finite and non-renewable. The total volume of workable mineral deposits represents only about one per cent of the earth's crust. Because the geological processes required to create and concentrate these minerals take millions of years, the rate of replenishment is infinitely small compared to current consumption rates.

As extraction continues, two major economic challenges arise:
- **Increasing costs:** Ores must be extracted from increasingly greater depths.
- **Decreasing quality:** The quality of available minerals tends to decline as high-grade deposits are exhausted.

## Strategies for Sustainable Use

To ensure mineral resources are used in a planned and sustainable manner, several approaches are necessary:
- **Technological advancement:** Constantly evolving improved technologies to allow for the use of low-grade ores at low costs.
- **Recycling:** Utilizing scrap metals and recycling existing metals.
- **Substitution:** Finding and using substitutes for traditional mineral resources.

**Key facts:**
- **Workable deposits:** Only ~1% of the earth's crust.
- **Resource type:** Finite and non-renewable.
- **Conservation methods:** Recycling, using scrap metals, technological evolution for low-grade ores, and substitution.
