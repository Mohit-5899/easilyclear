Manufacturing is the process of producing goods in large quantities by transforming raw materials into more valuable finished products. This activity falls within the secondary sector, where workers process primary materials into goods such as steel from iron ore, sugar from sugarcane, or paper from wood.

## Importance of Manufacturing

The manufacturing sector is considered the backbone of economic development for several reasons:

- **Modernizing Agriculture:** Manufacturing industries help modernize agriculture and reduce the heavy dependence of the population on agricultural income by providing employment in the secondary and tertiary sectors.
- **Poverty and Unemployment Eradication:** Industrial development is a precondition for reducing poverty and unemployment. This philosophy drives public sector industries and joint sector ventures, which also aim to reduce regional disparities by establishing industries in tribal and backward areas.
- **Trade and Foreign Exchange:** The export of manufactured goods expands trade and commerce, bringing in essential foreign exchange.
- **Economic Prosperity:** Countries that transform raw materials into a wide variety of high-value finished goods tend to be more prosperous.
- **Synergy with Agriculture:** Agriculture and industry are not exclusive; they move hand in hand. Agro-industries boost agricultural productivity by providing essential tools and inputs like irrigation pumps, fertilizers, pesticides, and machinery.

## Classification of Industries

Industries can be classified using various criteria to better understand their manufacturing processes:

**On the basis of source of raw materials:**
- **Agro-based:** Uses agricultural products (e.g., cotton, woollen, jute, silk, rubber, sugar, tea, coffee, and edible oil).
- **Mineral-based:** Uses mineral resources (e.g., iron and steel, cement, aluminium, machine tools, and petrochemicals).

**According to their main role:**
- **Basic or Key industries:** Supply their products as raw materials to manufacture other goods (e.g., iron, steel, and aluminium smelting).
- **Consumer industries:** Produce goods for direct use by consumers (e.g., sugar, toothpaste, paper, sewing machines, and fans).

**On the basis of capital investment:**
- **Small scale industry:** Defined by the maximum investment allowed on the assets of a unit. Currently, this limit is one crore rupees.

**On the basis of ownership:**
- **Public sector:** Owned and operated by government agencies (e.g., BHEL, SAIL).
- **Private sector:** Owned and operated by individuals or groups (e.g., TISCO, Bajaj Auto Ltd., Dabur Industries).
- **Joint sector:** Jointly run by the state and individuals or groups (e.g., Oil India Ltd.).
- **Cooperative sector:** Owned and operated by the producers/suppliers of raw materials, the workers, or both (e.g., sugar industry in Maharashtra, coir industry in Kerala).

**Based on bulk and weight of raw materials and finished goods:**
- **Heavy industries:** Use heavy raw materials (e.g., iron and steel).
- **Light industries:** Use light raw materials to produce light goods (e.g., electrical goods).

**Key facts:**
- **Manufacturing sector:** Known as the backbone of economic development.
- **Small scale industry investment limit:** ₹1 crore.
- **Textile industry uniqueness:** It is the only industry in India that is self-reliant and complete in the value chain, from raw material to high-value finished products.
