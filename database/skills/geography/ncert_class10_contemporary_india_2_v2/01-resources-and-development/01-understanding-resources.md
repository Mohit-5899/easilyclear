A resource is anything available in our environment that can be used to satisfy human needs, provided it meets three specific criteria: it must be technologically accessible, economically feasible, and culturally acceptable.

## The Transformation of Resources

Resources are not merely "free gifts of nature." Instead, they are a function of human activities. The process of transforming environmental materials into usable resources involves an interactive relationship between three key elements:

- **Nature:** Provides the raw materials and environment.
- **Technology:** The tools and methods used by humans to interact with and transform nature.
- **Institutions:** The structures created by humans to accelerate economic development.

Human beings are essential components of this process, as they use technology to transform materials into resources and establish institutions to manage them.

## Classification of Resources

Resources can be categorized based on four distinct criteria:

- **On the basis of origin:** Biotic and abiotic.
- **On the basis of exhaustibility:** Renewable and non-renewable.
- **On the basis of ownership:** Individual, community, national, and international.
- **On the basis of status of development:** Potential, developed stock, and reserves.

## Resource Development and Sustainability

Historically, the belief that resources were inexhaustible led to indiscriminate use, resulting in several global crises:

- **Depletion of resources:** Driven by the greed of a few individuals.
- **Social inequality:** The accumulation of resources in few hands has divided society into "haves" and "have-nots" (the rich and the poor).
- **Ecological crises:** Indiscriminate exploitation has caused global warming, ozone layer depletion, environmental pollution, and land degradation.

To ensure a sustained quality of life and global peace, equitable distribution and resource planning are essential. This leads to the concept of **sustainable development**, which dictates that economic development should occur without damaging the environment and should not compromise the needs of future generations.

## Global Initiatives: Rio Earth Summit and Agenda 21

In June 1992, more than 100 heads of state met in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil, for the first **International Earth Summit**. This summit addressed urgent problems regarding environmental protection and socio-economic development. Key outcomes included:

- The signing of the Declaration on Global Climatic Change and Biological Diversity.
- The endorsement of global Forest Principles.
- The adoption of **Agenda 21**, a declaration aimed at achieving global sustainable development through international cooperation to combat environmental damage, poverty, and disease. A major objective of Agenda 21 is that every local government should implement its own local agenda.

**Key facts:**
- **Resource criteria:** Must be technologically accessible, economically feasible, and culturally acceptable.
- **Rio Earth Summit:** Held in June 1992 in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil.
- **Agenda 21:** A declaration from the 1992 UNCED aimed at global sustainable development and local government action.
