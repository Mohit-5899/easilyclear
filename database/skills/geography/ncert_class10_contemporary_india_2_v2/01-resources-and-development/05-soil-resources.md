Soil is a vital living system and a fundamental natural resource that serves as the medium for plant growth and supports various living organisms. It is formed over millions of years through the interaction of relief, parent rock, climate, vegetation, and time.

## Soil Profile

A soil profile consists of several distinct layers:

- **Top soil:** The uppermost layer of the soil.
- **Subsoil:** Composed of weathered rocks, sand, silt, and clay.
- **Substratum:** Made of weathered parent rock material.
- **Unweathered parent bed rock:** The bottom-most layer of solid rock.

## Major Soil Types in India

India's diverse relief, landforms, and climatic realms have led to the development of several distinct soil types.

### Alluvial Soil
This is the most widespread and important soil type in India.
- **Distribution:** Covers the entire northern plains (deposited by the Indus, Ganga, and Brahmaputra river systems), extends through a corridor in Rajasthan and Gujarat, and is found in the eastern coastal plains (deltas of the Mahanadi, Godavari, Krishna, and Kaveri rivers).
- **Classification by Age:**
    - **Bangar (Old Alluvial):** Contains a higher concentration of *kanker* (calcium nodules).
    - **Khadar (New Alluvial):** Contains more fine particles and is more fertile than Bangar.
- **Characteristics:** Highly fertile and rich in potash, phosphoric acid, and lime. It is ideal for growing sugarcane, paddy, wheat, and other cereals and pulses.

### Black Soil
Also known as **regur soil** or **black cotton soil**, it is typical of the Deccan trap (Basalt) region.
- **Distribution:** Covers the plateaus of Maharashtra, Saurashtra, Malwa, Madhya Pradesh, and Chhattisgarh, extending southeast along the Godavari and Krishna valleys.
- **Characteristics:** Made of extremely fine, clayey material with a high capacity to hold moisture. It is rich in calcium carbonate, magnesium, potash, and lime, but generally poor in phosphoric content.
- **Behavior:** It becomes sticky when wet and develops deep cracks during hot weather, which aids in soil aeration.

### Red and Yellow Soils
- **Formation:** These soils develop due to the diffusion of iron in crystalline and metamorphic rocks. They appear red but look yellow when hydrated.
- **Distribution:** Found in the eastern and southern parts of the Deccan plateau, Odisha, Chhattisgarh, southern parts of the middle Ganga plain, and the piedmont zone of the Western Ghats.

### Laterite Soil
Derived from the Latin word *later* (brick), this soil forms under tropical and subtropical climates with alternate wet and dry seasons.
- **Formation:** The result of intense leaching caused by heavy rain.
- **Characteristics:** Generally acidic (pH < 6.0) and deficient in plant nutrients. It is humus-rich under forests but humus-poor in semi-arid environments.
- **Usage:** With proper conservation, it is useful for growing tea and coffee (Karnataka, Kerala, Tamil Nadu) and cashew nuts (Tamil Nadu, Andhra Pradesh, Kerala).

### Arid Soils
- **Characteristics:** Range from red to brown, are sandy in texture, and saline in nature. They lack humus and moisture due to high temperatures and evaporation.
- **Structure:** The lower horizons contain *kankar* due to increasing calcium content, which restricts water infiltration.

### Forest Soils
Found in hilly and mountainous areas with sufficient rain forests.
- **Texture:** Varies from loamy and silty in valley sides to coarse-grained on upper slopes.
- **Himalayan regions:** These soils are acidic with low humus content due to denudation in snow-covered areas.

**Key facts:**
- **Alluvial soil systems:** Indus, Ganga, and Brahmaputra.
- **Black soil alternative name:** Regur soil.
- **Laterite origin:** Latin *later*, meaning brick.
- **Soil fertility factors:** Colour, thickness, texture, age, and chemical/physical properties.
