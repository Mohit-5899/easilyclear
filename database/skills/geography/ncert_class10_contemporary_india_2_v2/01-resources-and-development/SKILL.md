This chapter explores the fundamental nature of resources, the critical need for sustainable development, and the complexities of resource planning within the diverse landscape of India.

## Understanding Resources

A resource is defined as anything available in our environment that can be used to satisfy human needs, provided it meets three criteria:
- It is **technologically accessible**.
- It is **economically feasible**.
- It is **culturally acceptable**.

Resources are not merely "free gifts of nature"; they are a function of human activities. Through an interactive relationship between nature, technology, and institutions, human beings transform environmental materials into resources to accelerate economic development.

## Sustainable Development and Conservation

The indiscriminate exploitation of resources has historically led to significant global issues, including:
- **Resource depletion** driven by individual greed.
- **Social inequality**, dividing society into "haves" and "have-nots."
- **Ecological crises**, such as global warming, ozone layer depletion, environmental pollution, and land degradation.

To combat these, the concept of **sustainable development** was introduced. This principle dictates that development should occur without damaging the environment and must ensure that present needs do not compromise the ability of future generations to meet their own needs.

Key milestones in global sustainability include:
- **The Brundtland Commission Report (1987):** Introduced the concept of sustainable development in the publication *Our Common Future*.
- **The Rio de Janeiro Earth Summit (1992):** The first International Earth Summit, which produced the Declaration on Global Climatic Change and Biological Diversity and adopted **Agenda 21** to combat environmental damage and poverty through global cooperation.

## Resource Planning in India

India possesses enormous diversity in resource availability, creating a need for strategic planning. Some regions are resource-rich but economically backward, while others are resource-poor but economically developed. Examples include:
- **Mineral-rich regions:** Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, and Madhya Pradesh.
- **Water-abundant but infrastructure-poor:** Arunachal Pradesh.
- **Energy-rich but water-deficient:** Rajasthan.
- **Culturally rich but resource-deficient:** Ladakh.

Effective resource planning in India involves a three-step process:
1. **Identification and inventory:** Surveying, mapping, and estimating the qualitative and quantitative aspects of resources.
2. **Planning structure:** Developing the necessary technology, skills, and institutional setups.
3. **Integration:** Matching resource development plans with overall national development goals.
