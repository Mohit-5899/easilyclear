Resource planning and sustainability are essential for managing India's diverse and unevenly distributed resource base to ensure balanced national development.

## Resource Planning in India

Resource planning is a complex process required to address the disparities between different regions. Some areas are self-sufficient, while others face acute shortages of vital resources. For example:
- **Mineral and coal-rich regions:** Jharkhand, Chhattisgarh, and Madhya Pradesh.
- **Water-abundant but infrastructure-poor regions:** Arunachal Pradesh.
- **Energy-rich but water-deficient regions:** Rajasthan (solar and wind energy).
- **Culturally rich but resource-deficient regions:** Ladakh (lacks water, infrastructure, and certain minerals).

Because some resource-rich regions remain economically backward while some resource-poor regions are economically developed, a balanced planning approach is necessary at national, state, regional, and local levels.

### The Process of Planning
Resource planning involves three key stages:
1. **Identification and inventory:** Surveying, mapping, and performing qualitative and quantitative estimation and measurement of resources across the country.
2. **Developing a planning structure:** Creating a framework endowed with the appropriate technology, skills, and institutional setup to implement development plans.
3. **Integration:** Matching resource development plans with overall national development plans.

Crucially, the mere availability of resources does not guarantee development. Resources contribute to progress only when accompanied by appropriate technological development, institutional changes, and high-quality human resources.

## Resource Conservation and Sustainable Development

Irrational consumption and the over-utilization of resources can lead to severe socio-economic and environmental problems. This necessitates resource conservation.

### Philosophical and Global Perspectives
- **Gandhian Philosophy:** Mahatma Gandhi emphasized that resources are meant "for everybody’s need and not for any body’s greed." He identified the exploitative nature of modern technology and selfish individuals as the root causes of global resource depletion, advocating for "production by the masses" rather than mass production.
- **Club of Rome (1968):** Advocated for resource conservation in a systematic way at the international level.
- **Schumacher (1974):** Reintroduced Gandhian philosophy in his book, *Small is Beautiful*.
- **Brundtland Commission Report (1987):** This seminal report introduced the concept of **Sustainable Development** as a means for resource conservation and was published in the book *Our Common Future*.
- **Earth Summit (1992):** Held in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil, this summit provided another significant contribution to global resource management.

**Key facts:**
- **Resource Planning Stages:** Identification/Inventory, Planning Structure, and National Integration.
- **Brundtland Commission (1987):** Introduced the concept of "Sustainable Development."
- **Earth Summit:** Held in Rio de Janeiro, Brazil, in 1992.
- **Key Requirement for Development:** Resources + Technology + Institutional changes + Human resource quality.
