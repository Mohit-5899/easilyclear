This chapter examines the availability, scarcity, and management of water resources in India. While water is a renewable resource through the hydrological cycle, the growing demand and mismanagement of freshwater have led to significant challenges for both rural and urban populations.

## Water Scarcity: Causes and Dimensions

Water scarcity is not merely a result of low rainfall or drought-prone regions; it is a complex issue driven by several factors:

- **Quantitative Scarcity:** This is caused by the over-exploitation and excessive use of water, often due to a large and growing population. 
    - **Agriculture:** As the largest consumer of water, irrigated agriculture expands to meet food demands, often leading to falling groundwater levels due to the use of private wells and tube-wells.
    - **Urbanization and Industrialization:** Rapidly growing urban centers and intensive industrialization exert immense pressure on freshwater resources. Industries require large volumes of water and hydroelectric power.
    - **Unequal Access:** Water is often distributed unequally among different social groups.
- **Qualitative Scarcity:** Even in areas where water is physically abundant, it may be unusable due to pollution. Contaminants include:
    - Domestic and industrial wastes.
    - Chemicals, pesticides, and fertilizers used in agriculture.

## Water Management and Conservation

To prevent ecological crises and ensure food security, India employs various management strategies, ranging from ancient traditions to modern government missions.

### Historical Context
India has a long history of sophisticated hydraulic structures, including:
- **Ancient Systems:** Dams, lakes, and irrigation systems were extensively built during the time of Chandragupta Maurya. Evidence of irrigation works exists in Kalinga (Odisha), Nagarjunakonda (Andhra Pradesh), and Kolhapur (Maharashtra).
- **Medieval Structures:** The 11th-century Bhopal Lake and the 13th–14th century Hauz Khas tank in Delhi (constructed by Allauddin Khilji) demonstrate long-standing water management traditions.

### Modern Management
- **Multi-purpose Projects:** Modern dams are designed for integrated uses, including irrigation, electricity generation, flood control, domestic/industrial water supply, recreation, inland navigation, and fish breeding. Examples include the Bhakra–Nangal project and the Hirakud project.
- **Government Initiatives:** 
    - **Jal Jeevan Mission (JJM):** Aims to provide every rural household with a regular supply of potable piped water (55 litres per capita per day).
    - **Atal Bhujal Yojana (Atal Jal):** Implemented in water-stressed Gram Panchayats to encourage community behavioral changes toward water conservation.

**Key facts:**
- **Major Water Consumers:** Irrigated agriculture is the largest consumer of water.
- **Jal Jeevan Mission Goal:** 55 litres of potable piped water per capita per day for rural households.
- **Multi-purpose Project Example:** The Bhakra–Nangal project is used for both irrigation and hydel power production.
