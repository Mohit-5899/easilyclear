Rainwater harvesting involves the collection and storage of rainwater for various uses, serving as a socio-economically and environmentally viable alternative to large multi-purpose projects. India has a long tradition of using diverse techniques to harvest rainwater, groundwater, river water, and flood water based on local ecological conditions.

## Traditional Harvesting Methods

Different regions in India have developed specialized methods to manage water based on their specific geography:

- **Hill and Mountainous Regions:** In the Western Himalayas, diversion channels known as ‘guls’ or ‘kuls’ are constructed for agriculture. These channels can lead to circular village tanks to release water as needed.
- **Flood Plains:** In the flood plains of Bengal, inundation channels are developed to irrigate fields.
- **Arid and Semi-Arid Regions:** 
    - **Khadins and Johads:** In regions like Jaisalmer and other parts of Rajasthan, agricultural fields are converted into rain-fed storage structures that allow water to stand and moisten the soil.
    - **Tankas:** In Bikaner, Phalodi, and Barmer, houses traditionally feature underground tanks called *tankas* to store drinking water. These are often built inside the main house or courtyard and can be as large as a room. In Phalodi, one such tank measured 6.1m deep, 4.27m long, and 2.44m wide.

## Rooftop Rainwater Harvesting

Rooftop harvesting is a common practice used to collect drinking water, particularly in Rajasthan and Shillong, Meghalaya. In Shillong, despite being near high-rainfall areas like Cherrapunjee, rooftop harvesting meets 15–25% of a household's total water requirement.

### The Process
1. **Collection:** Rain falls on sloping roofs and travels through pipes (often PVC).
2. **Filtration:** The water is filtered using sand and bricks.
3. **Storage/Recharge:** 
    - Water is directed to a sump for immediate use.
    - Excess water can be directed to a well to recharge underground water levels.
    - Alternatively, water can be recharged through hand pumps or abandoned dugwells.

**Note on Collection:** During the first spell of rain, water is usually not collected, as this initial flow serves to clean the rooftops and pipes.

## Bamboo Drip Irrigation

In Meghalaya, a 200-year-old system is used to tap stream and spring water using bamboo pipes. 

- **Mechanism:** Perennial springs on hilltops are diverted via gravity through bamboo pipes to lower reaches.
- **Flow Control:** The system uses channel sections and diversion units. The flow is controlled by manipulating pipe positions.
- **Efficiency:** Approximately 18–20 litres of water enters the system, is transported over hundreds of metres, and is reduced to a drip of 20–80 drops per minute at the plant site.

**Key facts:**
- **Tamil Nadu:** The first Indian state to make rooftop rainwater harvesting compulsory for all houses.
- **Palar Pani:** The term used in Rajasthan for rainwater, considered the purest form of natural water.
- **Gendathur (Karnataka):** A village where nearly 200 households use rooftop harvesting, collecting approximately 50,000 litres of water per house annually.
