While three-fourths of the Earth's surface is covered by water, only a small proportion is freshwater available for human use. This freshwater is replenished through the hydrological cycle via precipitation, surface runoff, and groundwater, making water a renewable resource. Despite this renewability, many regions face severe water scarcity.

## Causes of Water Scarcity

Water scarcity is not solely a result of low rainfall or drought-prone geography. It is a complex issue driven by both quantitative and qualitative factors.

### Quantitative Scarcity (Shortage of Volume)
Scarcity often arises from the over-exploitation and unequal distribution of available water:
- **Population Growth and Demand:** A large and growing population increases the demand for domestic use and food production.
- **Agricultural Over-exploitation:** Irrigated agriculture is the largest consumer of water. To increase food-grain production, farmers often use private wells and tube-wells, which can lead to falling groundwater levels and threaten food security.
- **Industrialization and Urbanization:** Intensive industrialization exerts heavy pressure on freshwater resources. Industries are significant water users and also rely on hydroelectric power.
- **Urban Lifestyles:** Rapidly multiplying urban centers with dense populations require vast amounts of water and energy. Many urban housing societies use private groundwater pumping devices, leading to the depletion of fragile local water resources.

### Qualitative Scarcity (Poor Water Quality)
An area may have ample water resources but still suffer from scarcity if the water is unfit for use. This occurs when water is polluted by:
- Domestic and industrial wastes.
- Chemicals, pesticides, and fertilizers used in agriculture.

## The Need for Conservation and Management

Effective water management is essential to prevent an ecological crisis and ensure the continuation of livelihoods. Conservation is necessary to:
- Safeguard public health from waterborne hazards.
- Ensure food security.
- Protect natural ecosystems from degradation.

To address these challenges, the Government of India launched the **Jal Jeevan Mission (JJM)**. The goal of this mission is to provide every rural household with an assured supply of potable piped water at a service level of 55 litres per capita per day on a regular, long-term basis.

**Key facts:**
- **Hydrological Cycle:** The process that ensures water is a renewable resource through precipitation, runoff, and groundwater recharge.
- **Largest Water Consumer:** Irrigated agriculture.
- **Predicted Scarcity:** By 2025, nearly two billion people are predicted to live in absolute water scarcity.
- **Jal Jeevan Mission (JJM) Target:** 55 litres of potable piped water per capita per day for rural households.
