Multi-purpose river projects involve the construction of dams to manage water resources through an integrated approach, combining various uses of impounded water to support national development.

## Understanding Dams

A dam is a barrier built across flowing water that obstructs, directs, or retards its flow. While the term "dam" often refers to the structure itself, it is also used to describe the resulting reservoir, lake, or impoundment. Most dams include a section known as a spillway or weir to manage the intermittent or continuous flow of water.

Dams are classified in several ways:
- **By structure and material:** Timber dams, embankment dams, or masonry dams.
- **By height:** Low, medium height, or high dams (also categorized as large or major dams).
- **By purpose:** Traditionally built for irrigation, but modern projects are "multi-purpose."

## Multi-Purpose Projects

After Independence, multi-purpose projects were viewed as vehicles for progress. Jawaharlal Nehru famously referred to them as the "temples of modern India" because they aimed to integrate agricultural development and the village economy with rapid industrialization and urban growth.

### Benefits and Uses
Modern dams serve multiple integrated functions, including:
- **Irrigation:** Providing water to agricultural fields.
- **Electricity generation:** Producing hydel power (e.g., the Bhakra–Nangal project in the Sutluj-Beas basin).
- **Water supply:** Meeting domestic and industrial needs.
- **Flood control:** Managing river flow (e.g., the Hirakud project in the Mahanadi basin).
- **Other uses:** Recreation, inland navigation, and fish breeding.

### Major Project Example: Sardar Sarovar Dam
Built over the Narmada River in Gujarat, this is one of India's largest water resource projects. It covers four states: Maharashtra, Madhya Pradesh, Gujarat, and Rajasthan. It is designed to provide water to drought-prone and desert areas, such as the Barmer and Jalore districts in Rajasthan.

## Controversies and Environmental Impact

In recent years, large dams have faced significant scrutiny due to several ecological and social issues:

- **River Health:** Damming affects natural flow, leading to poor sediment flow and excessive sedimentation at the bottom of reservoirs. This results in rockier stream beds and degraded habitats for aquatic life.
- **Fragmentation:** Dams fragment rivers, making it difficult for aquatic fauna to migrate for spawning.
- **Submergence:** Reservoirs created on floodplains submerge existing vegetation and soil, leading to decomposition.
- **Flooding and Erosion:** Ironically, sedimentation in reservoirs can trigger floods during excessive rainfall. These floods can cause extensive soil erosion and deprive floodplains of silt, a natural fertilizer.
- **Other Impacts:** Large projects have been linked to induced earthquakes, water-borne diseases, pests, and pollution.
- **Agricultural Changes:** Irrigation has shifted cropping patterns toward water-intensive commercial crops, which can lead to soil salinization.
- **Inter-state Disputes:** Diversion of water for multi-purpose projects can lead to conflicts between states, such as the Krishna-Godavari dispute involving Karnataka, Andhra Pradesh, and Maharashtra.

**Key facts:**
- **Bhakra–Nangal Project:** Uses water for both irrigation and hydel power in the Sutluj-Beas basin.
- **Hirakud Project:** Integrates water conservation with flood control in the Mahanadi basin.
- **Sardar Sarovar Project:** Spans Gujarat, Maharashtra, Madhya Pradesh, and Rajasthan.
- **Historical Context:** Sophisticated hydraulic structures (dams, canals, reservoirs) have existed in India since ancient times, including works during the Maurya period and the 11th-century Bhopal Lake.
