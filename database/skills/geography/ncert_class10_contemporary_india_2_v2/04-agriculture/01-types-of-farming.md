Agricultural cultivation methods vary significantly based on physical environments, technological knowledge, and socio-cultural practices. In India, these systems are categorized into three primary types: primitive subsistence, intensive subsistence, and commercial farming.

## Primitive Subsistence Farming

This type of farming is practiced on small patches of land using primitive tools such as hoes, *daos*, and digging sticks. It relies on family or community labor and is heavily dependent on the monsoon, natural soil fertility, and environmental conditions.

A key characteristic of this method is "slash and burn" agriculture. Farmers clear a patch of land and grow food crops to sustain their families. Once soil fertility decreases, they shift to a fresh patch of land, allowing nature to replenish the fertility of the previous plot through natural processes. Because farmers do not use modern inputs or fertilizers, land productivity remains low.

This method is known by various regional names:
- **North-eastern states (Assam, Meghalaya, Mizoram, Nagaland):** Jhumming
- **Manipur:** Pamlou
- **Bastar district (Chhattisgarh) and Andaman and Nicobar Islands:** Dipa
- **Madhya Pradesh:** Bewar or Dahiya
- **Andhra Pradesh:** Podu or Penda
- **Odisha:** Pama Dabi, Koman, or Bringa
- **Western Ghats:** Kumari
- **South-eastern Rajasthan:** Valre or Waltre
- **Himalayan belt:** Khil
- **Jharkhand:** Kuruwa

## Intensive Subsistence Farming

Intensive subsistence farming is practiced in areas where there is high population pressure on land. It is a labor-intensive system that utilizes high doses of biochemical inputs and irrigation to achieve higher production levels. 

In many parts of India, the "right of inheritance" causes land to be divided among successive generations, resulting in small, uneconomical land-holding sizes. Despite this, farmers continue to seek maximum output from their limited land due to a lack of alternative livelihoods.

## Commercial Farming

The primary goal of commercial farming is to achieve high productivity through the use of modern inputs, including:
- High Yielding Variety (HYV) seeds
- Chemical fertilizers
- Insecticides and pesticides

The degree of commercialization varies by region. For instance, rice is a commercial crop in Punjab and Haryana, whereas it serves as a subsistence crop in Odisha.

### Plantation Farming
Plantation is a specialized form of commercial farming where a single crop is grown over a large area. It acts as an interface between agriculture and industry, using capital-intensive inputs and migrant labor. The produce is typically used as raw material for industries. 

Key examples in India include:
- **Tea:** Assam and North Bengal
- **Coffee:** Karnataka
- **Other crops:** Rubber, sugarcane, and banana

Because production is market-oriented, plantations require a well-developed network of transport and communication to connect the farm to processing industries and markets.

**Key facts:**
- **Primitive tools:** Hoe, dao, and digging sticks.
- **Slash and burn names (Global):** Milpa (Mexico/Central America), Conuco (Venezuela), Roca (Brazil), Masole (Central Africa), Ladang (Indonesia), and Ray (Vietnam).
- **Intensive farming driver:** High population pressure and land fragmentation due to inheritance.
- **Plantation crops:** Tea, coffee, rubber, sugarcane, and banana.
