Agriculture is a primary economic activity in India, engaging approximately two-thirds of the country's population. It serves as a vital source of food grains and provides essential raw materials for various industries, while products such as tea, coffee, and spices are significant for export.

## Farming Systems

Agricultural practices in India vary widely based on environmental characteristics, technological knowledge, and socio-cultural practices. The following systems are currently practiced:

- **Primitive Subsistence Farming**: Practiced on small patches of land using traditional tools like hoes, daos, and digging sticks. This "slash and burn" method relies on monsoon rains and natural soil fertility. Once soil fertility declines, farmers move to a new patch to allow nature to replenish the land.
- **Intensive Subsistence Farming**: Common in areas with high population pressure on land. This is a labour-intensive system that utilizes high doses of biochemical inputs and irrigation to maximize production from limited landholdings.
- **Commercial Farming**: Characterized by the use of modern inputs, such as high-yielding variety (HYV) seeds, chemical fertilizers, insecticides, and pesticides, to achieve higher productivity.
- **Plantation**: A specialized form of commercial farming where a single crop is grown on a large area using capital-intensive inputs and migrant labor. These plantations often serve as an interface between agriculture and industry (e.g., tea, coffee, rubber, and sugarcane).

## Cropping Seasons

India follows three distinct cropping seasons:

1. **Rabi**: Sown in winter (October–December) and harvested in summer (April–June). Key crops include wheat, barley, peas, gram, and mustard.
2. **Kharif**: Grown with the onset of the monsoon and harvested in September–October. Major crops include paddy, maize, jowar, bajra, cotton, jute, and groundnut.
3. **Zaid**: A short summer season between the Rabi and Kharif seasons. It includes crops like watermelon, muskmelon, cucumber, and various vegetables.

**Key facts:**
- **Population engagement**: Two-thirds of India's population.
- **Rice production**: India is the second-largest producer of rice in the world, after China.
- **Rice requirements**: High temperature (above 25°C) and high humidity with annual rainfall above 100 cm.
- **Plantation examples**: Tea in Assam and North Bengal; coffee in Karnataka.
