India's agricultural landscape is defined by diverse cropping patterns shaped by varying soil, climate, and cultivation practices. Crops are categorized into three main seasons: **Rabi** (sown October–December, harvested April–June), **Kharif** (grown with the monsoon, harvested September–October), and **Zaid** (a short summer season between Rabi and Kharif).

## Food Crops

### Grains and Cereals
- **Rice:** The staple food for most Indians and the second largest producer globally after China. It is a Kharif crop requiring high temperatures (above 25°C), high humidity, and annual rainfall exceeding 100 cm. In low-rainfall areas, it relies on irrigation.
- **Wheat:** The second most important cereal and a major food crop in north and north-western India. It is a Rabi crop requiring a cool growing season, bright sunshine during ripening, and 50–75 cm of evenly distributed annual rainfall.
- **Millets:** Known as coarse grains, they are highly nutritious. 
    - **Jowar:** The third most important food crop by area and production; it is a rain-fed crop.
    - **Bajra:** Grows well in sandy and shallow black soils.
    - **Ragi:** A crop of dry regions, rich in iron, calcium, and micronutrients.
- **Maize:** Used for both food and fodder. It is a Kharif crop requiring temperatures between 21°C and 27°C, though in Bihar, it is also grown in the Rabi season.

### Pulses
India is the world's largest producer and consumer of pulses, which are essential protein sources for vegetarians. Most pulses (except arhar) are leguminous and help restore soil fertility by fixing nitrogen. Major varieties include tur (arhar), urad, moong, masur, peas, and gram.

### Other Food Crops
- **Sugarcane:** A tropical and subtropical crop that takes almost a year to grow. It requires a hot, humid climate (21°C–27°C) and annual rainfall between 75 cm and 100 cm. It is the primary source of sugar, gur (jaggery), khandsari, and molasses.
- **Oilseeds:** Cover approximately 12% of India's total cropped area. Major types include groundnut (a Kharif crop), mustard (Rabi), linseed (Rabi), and sesame (Kharif in the north, Rabi in the south).
- **Beverage Crops:** 
    - **Tea:** A plantation agriculture example and a labor-intensive industry. It requires tropical/sub-tropical climates with deep, fertile, well-drained soil rich in humus.
    - **Coffee:** Indian coffee, particularly the Arabica variety, is world-renowned. Cultivation is largely confined to the Nilgiri in Karnataka, Kerala, and Tamil Nadu.
- **Horticulture:** India is the second largest producer of fruits and vegetables globally. This includes tropical fruits (mangoes, bananas) and temperate fruits (apples, pears, apricots).

## Non-Food Crops
- **Rubber:** An equatorial crop grown in tropical/sub-tropical areas under special conditions. It requires a moist, humid climate with rainfall over 200 cm and temperatures above 25°C. It serves as a vital industrial raw material.

**Key facts:**
- **Rice status:** India is the 2nd largest producer in the world.
- **Sugarcane status:** India is the 2nd largest producer after Brazil.
- **Pulse status:** India is the world's largest producer and consumer.
- **Tea status:** India is the 2nd largest producer after China.
- **Groundnut status:** In 2020, India was the 2nd largest producer after China.
- **Horticulture status:** India is the 2nd largest producer of fruits and vegetables after China.
