Community participation in conservation involves local populations using traditional knowledge, social movements, and institutional frameworks to protect natural habitats and wildlife.

## Traditional Conservation and Sacred Groves

Many Indian communities practice nature worship, an age-old tribal belief that all natural creations must be protected. This has led to the preservation of "Sacred Groves"—patches of virgin forest left untouched by local people, where any interference is strictly banned.

- **Sacred Trees:** Various societies revere specific tree species.
    - The Mundas and Santhal of the Chota Nagpur region worship *mahua* (*Bassia latifolia*) and *kadamba* (*Anthocaphalus cadamba*).
    - Tribals in Odisha and Bihar worship tamarind (*Tamarindus indica*) and mango (*Mangifera indica*) trees during weddings.
    - Peepal and banyan trees are widely considered sacred across Indian society.
- **Wildlife Protection:** Sacred qualities are often ascribed to animals and landscapes.
    - Troops of macaques and langurs are frequently protected and fed around temples.
    - In Bishnoi villages in Rajasthan, animals such as blackbuck (chinkara), nilgai, and peacocks are treated as an integral part of the community and are not harmed.

## Social Movements and Community Action

Local communities often take direct action to resist environmental destruction or promote sustainable practices.

- **Chipko Movement:** Originating in the Himalayas, this movement successfully resisted deforestation and demonstrated that community-led afforestation using indigenous species can be highly effective.
- **Sariska Tiger Reserve:** In Rajasthan, villagers have utilized the Wildlife Protection Act to fight against mining activities to protect their habitats.
- **Bhairodev Dakav ‘Sonchuri’:** In the Alwar district of Rajasthan, inhabitants of five villages declared 1,200 hectares of forest as a protected area, establishing their own rules to prohibit hunting and prevent outside encroachment.
- **Sustainable Agriculture:** Groups like *Beej Bachao Andolan* (Tehri) and *Navdanya* have proven that diversified crop production is possible and economically viable without the use of synthetic chemicals.

## Joint Forest Management (JFM)

The Joint Forest Management (JFM) programme is a formal mechanism for involving local communities in the management and restoration of degraded forests.

- **Origins:** The state of Odisha passed the first resolution for JFM in 1988.
- **Mechanism:** JFM relies on the formation of local village institutions that undertake protection activities on degraded forest land managed by the forest department.
- **Benefits:** In exchange for their protection efforts, community members receive intermediary benefits, such as non-timber forest products and a share in the timber harvested through successful protection.

**Key facts:**
- **First JFM resolution:** Passed by Odisha in 1988.
- **Bhairodev Dakav ‘Sonchuri’ size:** 1,200 hectares in Alwar, Rajasthan.
- **Sacred tree species:** *Mahua*, *Kadamba*, Tamarind, and Mango.
- **Bishnoi villages:** Known for the protection of blackbuck, nilgai, and peacocks.
