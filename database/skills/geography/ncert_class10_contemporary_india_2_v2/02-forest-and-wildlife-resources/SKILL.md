This chapter explores the immense biological diversity of India and the critical importance of conserving our forest and wildlife resources to maintain the ecological systems that support human life.

## Biodiversity and Ecological Importance

Biodiversity, or biological diversity, refers to the vast array of wildlife and cultivated species that are diverse in form and function, yet closely integrated through complex networks of interdependencies. Humans are part of this complex ecological web and depend on it for essential life-support systems, including:

- **Air quality:** Re-created by plants, animals, and micro-organisms.
- **Water:** Purified through natural ecological processes.
- **Soil:** Maintained by biological activity to produce food.

Forests serve as primary producers within these systems, making them foundational to the survival of all living beings.

## Conservation Efforts in India

Due to the rapid decline in wildlife populations and forest cover, conservation has become essential to preserve genetic diversity and ecological stability. Key milestones in Indian conservation include:

- **The Indian Wildlife (Protection) Act (1972):** Implemented to protect habitats, ban hunting, and restrict wildlife trade. It established an all-India list of protected species.
- **Species-Specific Projects:** The government has launched various projects to protect threatened animals, such as the tiger, one-horned rhinoceros, Kashmir stag (hangul), various crocodiles (freshwater, saltwater, and Gharial), and the Asiatic lion.
- **Project Tiger (1973):** A major campaign launched after the tiger population dwindled significantly. India and Nepal together host approximately two-thirds of the world's surviving tiger population.
- **Expanding Scope:** Modern conservation has shifted from focusing on single species to protecting entire ecosystems, including insects (butterflies, moths, beetles) and plants.

## Classification of Forests

Forest resources in India are largely managed by the government and are categorized into three main types:

- **Reserved Forests:** Comprising more than half of the total forest land, these are considered the most valuable for conservation.
- **Protected Forests:** Accounting for almost one-third of the forest area, these are managed to prevent further depletion.
- **Unclassed Forests:** These consist of other forests and wastelands owned by government, private individuals, and local communities.

**Key facts:**
- **Permanent Forest Estates:** A term used to describe both Reserved and Protected forests.
- **Madhya Pradesh:** Holds the largest area of permanent forests (75% of its total forest area).
- **Tiger Reserves:** Notable examples include Corbett (Uttarakhand), Sunderbans (West Bengal), Bandhavgarh (Madhya Pradesh), Sariska (Rajasthan), Manas (Assam), and Periyar (Kerala).
